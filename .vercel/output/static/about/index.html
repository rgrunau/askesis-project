What is askesis?

The ancient Greeks had a word, askēsis, for the kind of disciplined practice that shapes a person from the inside. It began as a term from athletics — the daily repetition of physical training — and was gradually claimed by philosophers who saw that the same logic applied to the cultivation of character.

The Stoics practiced poverty days, cold baths, and fasting — not from asceticism for its own sake, but to test themselves against their own philosophy. Ignatius of Loyola gave the word to the Western spiritual tradition. It eventually gave us "ascetic." But at its root, askēsis was simply about practice: the belief that who you are is something you can work on.

This publication is built on that belief. It is an attempt to think seriously about self-cultivation — not as self-improvement in the contemporary sense, but in the older, more demanding sense: the formation of a person capable of living well.

Essays appear irregularly. They are long. They are meant to be read slowly.

The Askesis Project is kept alive by readers who find it worthwhile.
