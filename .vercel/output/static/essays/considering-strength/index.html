Considering Strength

October 31, 2025 · 3 min read · Read on Substack ↗

I started working with a coach couple of weeks ago. Someone I’ve respected and followed for a long time. They have an app, which I’ve been a member of off and on through a couple of iterations and when the opportunity came to work with him one-on-one I jumped at the chance.

During our opening consult call I was bragging about the weight I’ve lost over the last couple months doing the first program on the app, while also logging my food and counting calories. I assumed that we would continue on my path of hard work and calorie restriction. However, I was shocked when he suggested that I switch from losing weight to gaining it in the form of lean muscle mass.

I had confessed to him that part of my goal was in fact to gain upper body strength, but I figured we would do that through continued restriction and fat loss. I also confessed a desire not to be embarrassed when I take my shirt off because there’s nothing wrong with a little vanity to jump-start motivation.

What he pointed out, was that my plan put to keep losing weight was at odds with my desire to get stronger. If I truly wanted that change I was going to have to focus on building strength. This immediately made me uncomfortable.

I’ve never seen myself as a strong person. The reasons for this are many, most likely having to do with the fact that as a kid I was small and weak. Secondly I have the mental disposition of an endurance athlete and a history of pursuits that involve receptive motions for long periods of time. Sports where the idealized body type is on the smaller side. However, classical strength is not the only type of strength I’ve struggled with.

I also mean ability to express strength in my life outside of physical pursuits. I have traditionally struggled to keep my emotions balanced; I’ve struggled to stick to plans physical and financial, and I have often sacrificed long term gain for short term satisfaction. Those actions and beliefs have eroded the crucial attribute I’ve needed to truly transform myself: confidence.

He diagnosed this lack of confidence without me having to point it out, he could read it in my body language and in the way I described myself.

The new plan would be this: lift heavy things repeatedly. Build ability, confidence, and increased sensitivity through sustained effort. Let the physical work build what I’ve never given myself permission to have.

I’m going to focus on expanding capability and not aesthetics, through a more or less traditional body building repertoire of exercise. Three days a week of lifting heavy enough to trigger lean muscle growth. Running enough to keep and build on the aerobic base I already have, increasing my ability to move my body in novel ways, and a day to work on building capacity: those workouts where your lungs burn and you have to face the temptation to quit.

As always, the crux will be in moving that work into an internal transformation. As I’ve written in the past, this has always been the issue for me. So, for the next several months I’m going to pick up heavy things. I’m going to use the expression of strength to prove to myself that I can hold the line, that we can use right actions to feed right thoughts, to build the confidence I need to move into the next phase of my life; to commit to either starting my own business, or going back to school and changing careers. I’m also going to remember that this is hard work, that change is difficult and requires compassion.
