Reactionary Tendencies.

February 23, 2026 · 4 min read · Read on Substack ↗

To radically change who you are one has to move beyond the realm of aesthetic and focus on practices that help shape and turn one’s mind into the thing you want to become. Because, as I told Micheal, if I end this process still being the ass hat that I am currently then this whole project would have been a failure. For me, this means constantly looking at why I’m not only doing a particular training session, but also why I am training in the first place.

The goal of all this hypertrophy work was to build a more confident person than I was at the beginning. This means checking with the feeling of gaining muscle, and the knowledge that one is indeed doing the work. True confidence — I’m staring to learn — is not just the ability to feel like I belong in whatever room I am in, but also the ability to face what can be seen as a set back and makes adjustments when presented with new evidence without shattering your self worth or wanting the scrap the whole project all together. This week I learned I still have a fair amount of work to in building this new version of myself.

I have a strong reactionary streak running through me, and if I’m being totally honest, it is probably my most dominant personality streak. This shows up whenever I encounter a situation I view as a problem. Whenever this happens my mind jumps to the immediate response and tries to solve the problem as quickly as possible.

Occasionally this works out and I manage to solve a major problem at work through a series of quick and luckily clever solutions, but most often it works against me and I reach for a non-optimal solution. This trait/habit has lead to ruinous financial decisions (I’ve lost more than one good guitar this way and many a good bicycle), and ruined good jobs and relationships due to overcorrections. I most likely developed this trait as a survival mechanism, but it is something I’m still working to unlearn.

This trait showed itself this week after my DEXA scan showed that not only had I put on some muscle, but also a fair amount of fat gain, enough to have earned most of the fat loss that has occurred since October. I was mad and confused. Did I not work hard enough over previous six weeks? Obviously I ate too much, though looking back the scale didn’t tick up that much. “How did I fuck this up?” was actually my first thought. “I don’t want to be a chubby strong guy I lamented in an email; one I should have waited to send. I spent the next several hours inside my own head, ready to abandon the project and “work on something else for a while” In short I was ready to quit the path I’m on.

If one is to follow a physical philosophy, then it is also necessary that one reveal the inner workings of your mind. The mind and the body are not separate entities, but ones that need to be developed in tandem. Observing the quality of one’s thoughts while in the depths of a training session allows for the quit / not quit conversation to be a choice, not a reaction. It allows us to get one more rep when we think that we might be cooked. Outside the gym it is the difference between reacting and make a rash decision or responding to a situation base on one’s goals and the current conditions.

I didn’t leave my fugue state of rage and self loathing until I was, funnily enough, getting ready to eat. There in the shitty light of our kitchen light fixture it suddenly became clear that I was reenacting a well worn pattern of creating a crisis where there was none.

When zoomed out far enough progress always looks like up and to the right. In reality there are peaks and valleys — set backs. All that matters is how you respond to them. Yes, I’ve put some fat back on, but I am still at a lower body fat percentage than I was five months ago. It’s a thing to be managed, not a moral failure. What isn’t erased is the fact that I feel stronger and more capable than I did in October. I might be wrong, but I think that matters more. For me, the work will is not only picking shit up and putting it down, but taking a pause, a breath of three and not missing the forest for the trees. And paying attention. Always pay attention.
