A Barbell of Doubt

January 30, 2026 · 3 min read · Read on Substack ↗

The Askesis Project is a reader-supported publication. To receive new posts and support my work, consider becoming a free subscriber.

It’s been three months since I started lifting heavy and there is still a moment of trepidation every time I approach the barbell. Once the plates are loaded and it is time for the work to start I feel my pulse quicken and kernel of doubt rises in my chest. “Can I lift this weight?” “What happens if I fail, drop the bar on the gym floor, or my chest?” “Have I done the work to earn the this step up in weight?”

I’ve lived with some version of this doubt in all my endeavors. Every time I approach a new project at work, whenever it’s my call at the weekly jam and I kick off a tune I’ve practiced for months, and, every time I consider what I’m really doing with all these hours and reps

at the gym.

That sting of doubt has always been a sign for me to back off. It is the reason my hard drive is littered with half finished “good ideas” for software I thought I could turn into a business, and half spent notebooks can be found piled on various book shelves holding the seeds for novels and stories that will never be finished.

But, one of the many things that have changed over the last few months is that I can now feel the knot of doubt as it tightens, giving me a moment to turn it from a statement into a question.

Statements are damning because they are backed by an imperative. It feels like you couldn’t possibly do one more rep, or write one more word. “I can’t do another one” is what often runs through my head before repacking the bar. This is limiting and prevents forward progress. I’ve quit, which is worse than failing.

A question however is an opportunity to deepen your knowledge of yourself and what you’re capable of accomplishing. That feels scary because it involves stepping into the unknown and leaving your comfort zone. We’re all comfortable with the space and view of self currently poses. We might be miserable, but contentment isn’t a necessity component of comfort.

When posed as question, “I can’t do one more rep.” becomes “Can I do one more rep?” You might not be able to, and that ’s fine. The fine for dropping the weight on the floor, or asking for a spot is $0. The wealth gained from asking the question and moving beyond where you currently sit is worth more than gold.

More importantly, engaging with doubt is one of the main mechanisms of building a self that is capable and confident. Doubt is the seed that grows your faith in your ability to change.
