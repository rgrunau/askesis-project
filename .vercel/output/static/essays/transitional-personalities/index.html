Transitional Personalities

May 28, 2026 · 3 min read · Read on Substack ↗

A couple weekends ago I got together with a group of guys I’ve been meeting up with for the last nine years for a weekend of music and, for some, mild debauchery. We do this at least once a year and, given the frequency of the gathering it is sometime hard to remember the names of some of the newer folks or for them to remember you. I don’t have the biggest personality and I’m actually pretty low key at this event and I’ve learned to give a little latitude when someone spaces on my name. If I can remember to give them the correct name.

I’d just arrived at the house where I met Jim in the kitchen and gave a wave to Dan as he headed over to where Jim and and I were catching up.

“I’m sorry, remind me of your name again?” Dan asked.

“Robert” I said confidently.

“Robert, who the hell is Robert?” Jim jumped in.

I paused for a moment and laughed, “Bob. Sorry, you caught me between personalities.”

This is the second time I’ve recently caught myself in this gap between the person I deem myself to have been and the person I am becoming. Last month, while dropping my daughter off at a friend’s birthday I introduced myself to the kid’s grandma as Robert, but five minutes later I was introduced to someone else as “Bob” and a few minutes after that I introduced myself to the kid’s Grandpa as Bob. When I made my exit from the party there were both declarations of “See you later Bob” and “Nice to meet you Robert”. I assumed my failed Irish goodbye generated some confusion.

For a long time the space was more clearly defined. There were contexts in which I was Bob and others where I was seamlessly Robert, now they are getting more and more confused.

When I became a software engineer six years ago I made the conscious decision to make Robert my professional persona. I was starting something new, a career yes but I didn’t really had one of those before becoming a programmer. The new job was my chance to be someone different, but I didn’t understand the mechanics of change back then, and becoming that other person is taking some time.

Bob has been my main identity since the second grade when I stopped going by “Bobby”. I was eight then. I’m forty-six now. That’s a long time.

Bob is the person with the negative self view. The one who was primarily ruled by whatever winds of passion were blowing that day. He is rash, quick to anger, and seeks whatever raft he could cling to escape discomfort. Bob was focused on feeding his immediate desire, with no regard for the future.

Who is Robert?

I don’t know yet actually. He’s still being birthed, or forming, or whatever language one wants to use for this kind of transformation. “… this is the longest in my adult life I’ve gone without thinking about suicide.” I wrote to a mentor recently.

What I do know is that in this moment I feel stuck between the two personalities and I get caught out. When I want to be Robert, Bob will show up. Some weeks it’s a battle, but I can also discover the loop I’m caught in, take pause, and re-orient toward the person I want to be.

Maybe I need to read about shadow work next. Or I just need to keep on trying to be Robert a little more every day.
