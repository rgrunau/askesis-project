Some Guidelines for Living Life from This Day Forward

November 27, 2024 · 5 min read · Read on Substack ↗

I spent the days after the election, not in a state of shock, but one of anger—and not at the people one might expect me to be mad at. By Friday, I had emerged from the haze that strong emotions elicit. I decided that while I couldn't control the election outcome, I could control how I move through the world.

It was in that light that I developed the ten guidelines below. I'm unsure if they'll be helpful to my readers, but I believe there are some universal insights here. You can't control other people or completely control your thoughts, but I think these might help you navigate life.

Quick bit of housekeeping before we continue with these guidelines. This Substack is (at the time of this writing) free. But if you’d like to support this work, please consider sharing with someone(s). Thanks!

1. Remember, You Are Not Your Thoughts

Some years ago, I reluctantly accepted that free will, as we typically understand it, is an illusion. I first encountered this idea through Sam Harris, but Robert Sapolsky has done extensive recent work on this subject. Both are worth listening to on this hard-to-swallow pill.

This insight began the work of separating myself from my thoughts, including the realization that the "self" I view doesn't truly exist. As someone who experiences depression, low self-esteem, and occasional suicidal ideation, this understanding has been incredibly freeing—when I remember its truth.

2. Meditate Throughout the Day

This action—or more precisely, lack of action—is how you start to implement the first guideline. After nearly six years of consistent practice, I'm learning that ten minutes once or twice a day is necessary but not sufficient. One also needs to take moments throughout the day to break the spell of thought.

I'm improving at this practice. Most days, I can't fully reach the emptiness that runs through all phenomena, but at the very least, it gives me a moment to connect with my breath and realize I'm not a mind carried by a body, but a mind-body.

3. Write to Clarify Your Thinking

Writing your thoughts and feelings on any subject is the first step to testing those thoughts. Keeping a journal in my younger years carried me through difficult times and was ultimately what challenged my previous belief systems.

Writing, especially by hand, gives you a chance to clarify your arguments. At its best, you might uncover what you truly believe. At the very least, you'll strengthen your reasoning when debating with friends, family, and online bots (joking, don't argue with bots).

4. Treat Everyone with Kindness, Compassion, and Respect

If you are not your thoughts, then other people are also not their thoughts. In forty-five years of life, I've yet to meet a person who wasn't damaged in some way. Everyone has good and bad moments, and everyone wants to be loved and respected.

Give that respect to others, because too many cannot find the strength to give it to themselves. This is especially true when you disagree with someone. You'll never truly understand their beliefs and motives if you're preparing to "dunk" on them.

You don't have to like everyone, but offer a basic level of respect, even in the heat of an argument. For nearly two decades, we've seen a general degradation in how people treat each other, largely because most interactions now occur online, where we don't have to look each other in the eye.

5. Move Your Body Everyday

There's complex science about the default mode network and brain chemistry that I won't pretend to fully understand. All I know is that any day I get out and move—even just a walk through the neighborhood—didn’t solve my problems, but it has always given me a bit of distance from them.

6. Drink Alcohol Less Often

It's clear that alcohol is essentially poison. While not everyone needs to quit entirely, everyone can benefit from drinking less. Too many of us use it as a coping mechanism, but in truth, you're most likely running from the things that are bothering you.

7. Always Verify If Something Is Actually True

In one way or another, all these guidelines relate to the first one. When lost in the dream of our own thoughts, it's easy to confuse what we think is true versus what is actually true.

In his book "The Righteous Mind", Jonathan Haidt highlights research showing our minds have visceral reactions to moral beliefs, with our thinking mind then generating justifications for those views. When you have strong feelings about an issue you've just heard about, examine those feelings. Look for their source and recognize when you're creating a fiction.

8. Tell the People Closest to You That You Love Them

This might sound trite, but it's true: You don't know when you or your loved ones might leave this world. Ensure they know they are loved. At a certain point in my life, someone telling me they loved me was the only thing that kept me going.

9. Allow Your Views to Be Challenged

Refer to guidelines 2, 3, and 7. Test your views, listen to alternative arguments, and try to understand why others might hold different perspectives. Be willing to change your mind when presented with evidence.

10. Wait at least 24 Hours Before Making Financial Decisions

I struggle with financial decisions the way some people struggle with drugs and alcohol. I didn’t make myself this way, I definitely don’t want to be like this when it comes to dealing with money.

I’ve bought lots of dumb shit. I’ve returned more shit than I’d ever care to admit to anyone. I’ve experienced more stress about money than anything else in my life. A lot of suffering can be avoided or alleviated by just delaying an action that at first taste is satisfying.
