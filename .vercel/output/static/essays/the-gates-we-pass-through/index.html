The Gates We Pass Through

March 30, 2026 · 4 min read · Read on Substack ↗

For the past several weeks I’ve been a drift in a state of low grade nihilism, the likes of which I haven’t experienced since I lost my faith over twenty years ago. I spend most of my day not interacting with my co-workers, but with an Ai Agent who does a majority of the problem solving; the one fun thing about the career I never wanted is now done by a machine that is faster and better than I could ever be. As a co-worker said in a recent meeting about Ai: “You’re going to have to find a different way to get your dopamine”.

Instead of seeing this as a crisis to be solved I’m starting to see this kind of nihilism as a gate a person has to pass through when the architecture of their meaning begins to collapse or when they experience a loss of agency. Both of these factors can cause a person to ask “What’s the point?”.

The fact is, a lot of my life is in flux at the moment. As I hinted at above, my job is changing. The thing that vaulted me to the middle class will most likely be automated to the point where what was done by a team of software engineers will be done by one technical PM, and I’m having hard time committing to a new career path.

On a deeper level I’m suspended between the person I was at this time last year and the person I’m becoming. This is a dangerous time where a person can back slide to their old ways or continue the narrower path they’ve decided to walk (it isn’t necessarily a straight one). I have enough distance from that person that the degree to which I’ve changed is marked more by what I no longer do, and less by the healthier habits I’ve adopted. I can see still see that old version of Bob in the rearview mirror and he’s still waiving at me as drive away.

As I see it, the crux of the problem is that I have to learn how this version of Robert makes meaning and claims his slice of agency in this world. For better or worse, the negative view of self I carried for most of my forty-six years was a source of meaning. The way we define ourselves shapes and gives meaning to our world. This negative view created world where I could not get the things I wanted, and never allowed me ask what it was I really wanted. A place where I was doomed to enact the same bad choices again and again. I didn’t trust myself enough to enact change and I didn’t trust myself enough to advocate for myself, or see clearly enough what I was capable of bringing not just to a job, but my own life.

I had no agency and because of messages I internalized and the story I told myself about what I was actually capable of. It’s easier to stay where you are, than it is to accept responsibility for who you can be.

Before one can truly decide what their meaning of life is one has have enough trust in themselves to cross over to a better world, one a little less affected by the world around it, and the pulls of culture and society. Creating your own meaning and defining your own ethics is a high wire act. It requires trust and faith, which are built by consistently doing the practices that can make you a better version of yourself.

In classic collection of Zen koan’s titled “The Gateless Gate” the complier of the collection, Mumon says that the first case - the infamous Mu - is the gateless barrier of the zen sect. This sanga case is one of first ones a student is given to work with, and it is one they come back to over and over again, each visit revealing a deeper level of the practice.

This is how I view this state of philosophical questioning I’m in now. The feeling of meaninglessness is one I’ve had before, but rather than giving into it, I’m trying to be curious. Can I use my current state of “There is no meaning” to finally drive around the bend and leave my past selves behind to rest. They’ve done their work, and it’s time to start mine.
