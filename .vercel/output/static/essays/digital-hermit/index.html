Digital Hermit

January 22, 2025 · 5 min read · Read on Substack ↗

I deleted most of my social media accounts on the 8th of January, a day after Meta's announced policy changes. Not because of the announcement itself, but because I finally came to the realization that I needed to rescue my most precious resources — my attention and time. The addictive nature of these platforms coupled with my predisposed tendencies toward over consumption and low self-esteem was becoming too much for me. Instead of longing for a different life, I decided to make one.

A few accounts remain: this Substack, a mostly unused Bluesky account, and LinkedIn — an unfortunate necessity for finding work. I still have two Twitter accounts floating in digital limbo: one I couldn't figure out how to delete, and another I can't even access (I've logged in maybe twice since 2021). The remaining platforms don't concern me much; they lack the addictive pull that Instagram and Facebook had, and might even prove useful as syndication channels for this newsletter. Bluesky and LinkedIn simply don't have the same gravitational force on my attention.

I told no one of my decision at first. The whole thing was an impulsive act, proving that even our least desirable traits can occasionally serve noble purposes. My modest reach on these platforms didn't warrant any grand announcements or posturing. The truth is, few would notice my departure — my posting frequency had dwindled over the years, and my recent posts attracted diminishing engagement which sadly left me feeling sad and dejected.

"In the week leading up to deleting my accounts, I felt like I was on my Instagram constantly. My screen time was much higher than average, and despite Apple's flimsy screen time limits, thirty or forty minutes would pass. This was happening several times a day. Any time I couldn't immediately figure something out at work, or a random thought would bubble up, of course it would lead me back to the phone. This pattern would continue throughout the day, long past the fifteen minutes I had allotted to Instagram.

But once I was in IG and scrolling mindlessly through my feed, I'd get distracted by someone's thread hot take, get lost there for a while before realizing that I was looking for some dumbass fit pic, or plumbing the depths of a musician's feed, looking for that one time Joe K Walsh played Bill Frisell's 'Verona' six fucking years ago.

The fact that this type of compulsive scrolling is classic avoidance behavior. I would be keenly aware of that as soon as I would return from my addled state. The problem here is that whatever work or life anxiety sent me to the internet would most likely be exacerbated by my scrolling. The work problem still needed to be solved; the data still needed engineering, but now I had less time to do my task, and I would have no idea what it was I was working on before. The madness had to stop, and there was only one way to put an end to it.

But this wasn't the only reason I finally got off the treadmill.

For me, social media has been a big bummer since right before the pandemic started back in 2020. To my view, it had been experiencing a decline since 2015, but the precipitous decline really kicked in when COVID started. By 2024, I was seeing more takes from people who just don't have enough information to even have a take. I was seeing less and less of the cool things my friends were doing. I was seeing more ads for apps promising to get me so ripped my wife wouldn't be able to keep her hands off me. I was seeing more posts from aggregation accounts that produce no actual content but mine that content of others to turn a profit and probably just keep you distracted.

Back in the late aughts/early 2010s, social media actually connected you with people. I met some cool-ass people through Twitter and Instagram. I even became friends with some of them. It opened up writing opportunities and put me on a path to do that until my penchant for self-destruction and life in general kicked in. You have to look a lot harder for that type of stuff now, and the juice, it ain't worth the squeeze.

Or maybe it's just that I'm old now and I know that the whole social media enterprise is built on a tacit agreement that in exchange for your friend liking your shitty, improperly framed photo, you will also like their photo, or endorse their caption. Furthermore, the two of you will do it in the laziest way possible, by pressing your finger on that little heart icon. Your liking of a friend's post does nothing, zero. It's not support. It's fake. And we all suffer the consequences. Despite all the followers and shares and likes, as a society we are less connected to our communities, mostly sad, and lonesome. It's little wonder the world feels like it is falling apart.

All this has me feeling like the life of a hermit has a lot of merits to it. However, my time for being a hermit has passed. Twenty-some years ago I considered joining a Zen monastery, but then I met a girl, liked going to bars too much, and generally enjoyed being a shithead. Now, I have a family I love, a job that keeps my bills paid and my insurance covered, and I don't want to give any of that up. Maybe twenty or thirty years from now the opportunity to be an actual hermit will present itself again*. Till then, I'll settle for being a digital hermit.

*In fact, I've told my daughter not to send me to an assisted living facility when my time comes, but to find me a cave and bring me a cooler of food once a week. I will find a way to make this utterance legally binding.
