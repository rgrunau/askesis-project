Discipline Doesn't Just Transfer

September 5, 2025 · 4 min read · Read on Substack ↗

Fifteen years ago I had a therapist named Andrew, who I really liked. I was in a time of transition and struggling to make any headway on behaviors that were holding me back (and would continue to hold me back for years to come). I enjoyed working with Andrew because he was willing to come back at me with the same level of intensity that I brought to our sessions. Unafraid to call out my self delusions in a way that have made other therapists blush.

I can furnish multiple examples of this from the several months we spent working together before I left Portland, and I’m sure Andrew’s name will make his way into future posts, but for the last couple weeks I’ve had one particular session floating around in my head.

Training, and bike racing were frequently part of our discussions. For several years I had been using hard training sessions as a way of paying penitence for my maladaptive behaviors. At the time I was struggling to do even the most basic thing to move my life forward and, to be frank, I wasn’t living with a lot of integrity.

At the same time I was living under the assumption that my ability to go hard during a training session and intentionally subject my self to pain and suffering would somehow push me past my current state and into a new one. I’m sure I was in the middle of delivering a sermon on the subject when he stopped me.

“Wait. Why do you feel like you have to go so hard when you’re working out? ”

“Two reasons: first I feel like I deserve the pain, that it’s a form of paying for the things I do wrong, for how I treat people. The second is that I believe that if I push myself hard enough, that discipline will flow down into other aspects of my life.”

I remember him erupting in laughter.

When his laughter subsided he looked up at me and said “That’s total bullshit” before starting to chuckle to himself again. “Just waiting for it to happen isn’t going to work”

Crossing my arms I tried to sink into the leather arm chair I sat in during our sessions. A period of silence passed as I struggled for a witty retort, or even just a simple “fuck you”. If my gospel was true, why hadn’t the diligent years of training resulted in any true breakthroughs? Had any of the people I admired broken through using the same methods? I was clinging, but I could feel the theory breaking down.

My thinking that I would just become more disciplined about money, more honest in my dealings with others and myself was as magical as the belief that giving the wealthy massive tax breaks will allow their wealth to trickle down to the rest of us. Things don’t just happen, you have to make them happen through application; or taxes 😉

That day in Andrew’s office hit me again as I sat on the astro turf floor of my local 24 hour fitness during the rest period of a difficult work out. I had just scrawled a barely legible 2, or was it a 5, in my notebook to denote the number of calories I’d burned on the SkiErg during the last round. An errant hair, long and brown had found its way from the floor and into the long piece of the pen cap meant to lock the pen into one’s pocket.

I pushed it aside and in the seconds before the bell marking the next round of work I realized the missing variable in my theory those many years ago: you have to work to transfer the lesson.

Insight is easy to come by. All the disciplined training was never just going to trickle down to the places where it mattered. Deep down I’ve always known that effort could change me, but what I’ve most often lacked is the bravery to ask: how can I apply this discipline elsewhere? The answer to that question will inevitably bring up more questions, and isn’t that the way it should be?
